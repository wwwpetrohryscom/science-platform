The greenhouse-gas concentration record is, by some distance, the cleanest indicator in the climate dataset. The atmospheric mole fraction of CO₂ in a clean-air sample is something a calibrated instrument can measure to a precision of about 0.1 parts per million. The same is true to comparable precision for methane and nitrous oxide. The long-term trends are not contested at the measurement level — they are read off the instruments.

What is worth understanding is how the global records get built from individual site measurements, what the major source agencies are, and what the concentration trends do and do not tell you on their own. The framework piece — what concentrations mean for [radiative forcing](/en/ecology/climate-change/greenhouse-gases-and-radiative-forcing) and ultimately for temperature — is handled separately in this cluster.

## The Mauna Loa baseline and the global network

The longest continuous CO₂ record began at Mauna Loa Observatory in 1958, established by Charles Keeling and continued today by the NOAA [Global Monitoring Laboratory](https://gml.noaa.gov/) in cooperation with Scripps Institution of Oceanography. The site was chosen because it sits in the middle of the Pacific at 3,400 m elevation, exposed to well-mixed background air for most of the year.

A single site, however good, is not a global mean. The NOAA GML operates a global Cooperative Air Sampling Network of around 60 background sites, with weekly flask sampling shipped to the central lab for analysis on a common instrument and a common calibration scale. The combination produces the global mean concentration that headline numbers refer to. The same network measures methane, nitrous oxide, sulfur hexafluoride, and the major halocarbons.

The complementary international product is the [World Meteorological Organization Greenhouse Gas Bulletin](https://wmo.int/), which combines data from the WMO Global Atmosphere Watch programme and produces an independent global mean. The two products are calibrated against the same gravimetric standards and agree to within stated uncertainties.

For column-integrated rather than surface measurements, satellites have a complementary role. JAXA's GOSAT, NASA's OCO-2 and OCO-3, and ESA's Sentinel-5P (for methane and minor gases) provide spatial coverage that surface networks cannot. The satellite products are calibrated against the surface network through the Total Carbon Column Observing Network (TCCON) and are reviewed alongside the surface record by the [Copernicus Climate Change Service](https://climate.copernicus.eu/).

## What the records show

The annual mean atmospheric CO₂ concentration has risen monotonically since the start of the Mauna Loa record. The pre-industrial baseline of about 280 parts per million is established from Antarctic ice-core records that extend the concentration history back roughly 800,000 years. Recent annual means exceed 420 ppm — higher than any point in the ice-core record and, according to longer geochemical reconstructions reviewed in [IPCC AR6 WG1](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/), almost certainly the highest in several million years.

Methane and nitrous oxide trends are also positive, with methane showing the most variable growth rate of the three. The early 2020s have seen above-long-term-average methane growth, and source attribution — whether the increase reflects wetland response to a warming climate, agricultural emissions, fossil-fuel system leakage, or a combination — is an active research area drawing on isotopic and satellite-column work documented in [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/)-indexed literature and in GML source-apportionment publications.

The seasonal cycle in CO₂ is the other feature of the record worth noting. Northern Hemisphere terrestrial vegetation draws down CO₂ during spring and summer and releases it during autumn and winter; the global mean record shows a clear seasonal wave on top of the long-term trend. The amplitude of that seasonal cycle has grown over the past several decades — a separately tracked indicator that informs the [carbon-cycle feedbacks](/en/ecology/climate-change/carbon-cycle-feedbacks) literature.

## The Annual Greenhouse Gas Index

For an operational summary of the *direct* radiative forcing component, the NOAA Global Monitoring Laboratory publishes the [Annual Greenhouse Gas Index](https://gml.noaa.gov/aggi/) (AGGI). The AGGI reports the year-over-year change in the direct radiative forcing of the five main long-lived greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide, CFC-12, CFC-11) plus 15 minor halogenated gases, indexed to 1990 = 1.0.

The AGGI exists to be the unambiguous single-number version of the gas record. It does not include aerosols — which carry the larger uncertainty in total anthropogenic forcing — and it does not include the slow climate response. It does isolate the part of the radiative-forcing trajectory that is directly attributable to measured concentration changes. When a discussion calls for one number that the entire climate community will agree on, the AGGI is the right one to cite.

## Reading concentration data well

Three distinctions repay attention.

**Concentration is not emission.** Atmospheric concentration is the result of cumulative emissions minus cumulative removals (ocean uptake, terrestrial uptake, chemical sinks). The growth rate is what relates concentration to current-year net flux; the integrated record is what relates it to historical emissions. The carbon-cycle inputs that NOAA and the [Copernicus Climate Change Service](https://climate.copernicus.eu/) draw on translate between the two using carbon-cycle models. A change in the growth rate does not by itself separate "emissions changed" from "sinks changed" — both contribute, and decomposing them is its own research question covered in [carbon-cycle feedbacks](/en/ecology/climate-change/carbon-cycle-feedbacks).

**Concentration is not radiative forcing.** Per-molecule effectiveness depends on band saturation. The relationship between CO₂ concentration and forcing is approximately logarithmic over the relevant range — covered in [greenhouse gases and radiative forcing](/en/ecology/climate-change/greenhouse-gases-and-radiative-forcing). Reading concentration trends without that translation overstates the linear-in-ppm impact and understates the time-integrated commitment.

**Concentration is not temperature.** The temperature response to a given concentration depends on climate sensitivity and on the time the system has had to respond. AR6 assesses likely equilibrium climate sensitivity at 2.5–4 °C per CO₂ doubling. The committed warming from concentrations already in the atmosphere is larger than what has been realized to date.

## Limitations and methodology notes

Concentration records carry small but documented limitations.

**Site representativeness.** Background sites are chosen to avoid local pollution sources; they represent the well-mixed atmosphere, not urban or industrial conditions. Urban CO₂ studies use a separate measurement design. The global mean is a global mean — it is not what a measurement next to a freeway will read.

**Calibration scales.** All major operational gas records trace to gravimetric reference materials maintained at NOAA GML and at the comparable European reference labs. Calibration scale revisions are documented in the technical literature; they affect the absolute number by amounts that are small relative to the trend but matter for cross-product comparisons.

**Pre-industrial baseline.** The 280 ppm pre-industrial CO₂ value is established from Antarctic ice-core records (EPICA Dome C and others), with cross-checks from Law Dome and Greenland cores for the more recent centuries. The ice-core record has its own age-model uncertainty for the deepest layers, but the modern-era baseline is well-constrained.

**Satellite biases.** Column-integrated satellite measurements are sensitive to aerosol scattering, cloud contamination, and surface albedo. The OCO-2 and TCCON bias-correction pipelines are documented in the mission literature; satellite products are most reliable when used alongside surface records rather than in isolation.

## What this indicator does and does not do

The concentration record is the strongest piece of physical evidence in climate science. It is also the indicator that demands the most careful framing, because its very precision tempts overreading. CO₂ at 420 ppm is a measurement of a thing; it is not by itself a forecast of what the climate system will do. That forecast requires the [climate models](/en/ecology/climate-change/climate-models-projections-uncertainty) and the [forcing translation](/en/ecology/climate-change/greenhouse-gases-and-radiative-forcing) to do their work.

Read alongside [ocean heat content](/en/ecology/climate-change/ocean-heat-content-indicators), the [Earth energy imbalance](/en/ecology/climate-change/climate-indicators-earth-system-monitoring), and the temperature and sea-level records, the concentration trends are one constraint in a system the other indicators have to satisfy. That is how the indicator framework holds together.
